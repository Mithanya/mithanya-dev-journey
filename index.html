Still Not Placed in 2026. I Have Stopped Pretending It Does Not Hurt.

Not advice. Not inspiration. Just what this phase actually feels like from the inside — written honestly, without editing out the uncomfortable parts.

MM
Mithanya Murugesan · March 21, 2026 · 6 min read

"Why are you still not placed?"

My relative asked me this last month at a family function. Casually. The way you ask someone if they have eaten. Like the answer would be quick and we would all move on.

I said something. Smiled a little. Changed the topic. Went home.

That was three weeks ago. The question is still here.

It has this strange quality to it. You answer it in the moment and think you are done with it. But it comes back at 11 at night when you are trying to sleep. It comes back in the morning before you have fully woken up. And then someone asks again the following week and the whole thing starts over exactly where it left off.

I am a final-year student. 8th semester. I have attended interviews. Sat in waiting rooms with a printed resume and slightly cold hands. Gone home and waited for emails that never came. I am still not placed. Writing that sentence out feels heavier than I expected it to.

I went looking for honest writing about this experience. Everything I found was a success story or a tips article. Nobody was writing about what it actually feels like to be inside this phase. In the middle of it. Without knowing how long it lasts.

So I decided to write it myself.

What Placement Pressure in Final Year Actually Does to You

It is not one big thing. That would almost be easier to deal with.

It is the neighbor asking the same question every time you walk to the gate. It is your mother staying quiet — which is somehow heavier than if she said something. It is the group chat you hesitate to open because you do not know what news is waiting there today. It is your own brain at 2 in the morning running through a list of things you should have done differently six months ago.

At some point the external pressure and the internal pressure become the same thing. You stop needing someone else to say it. You are already saying it to yourself, in a voice that sounds uncomfortably like your own.

"Job didn't come yet?" — Four words. Said in ten seconds. Stays with you for three days.

I have started calculating before I step outside. Who might be in the street. Whether the longer route is worth it today. Whether I have the energy for a particular conversation or whether I simply do not.

I did not expect this phase to feel like this. I thought it would feel like frustration. Not this specific kind of tired.

Watching Friends Get Placed While You Are Still Waiting

My friends are getting placed. Some got placed months ago. They share photos from their offices and I look at those photos and feel two completely different things at once.

I feel genuinely happy for them. That is real. These are people I like. They worked hard for it.

And at the same time — something else. Not jealousy exactly. More like watching everyone board a bus that you also had a ticket for, and the door closing before you reached it, and standing on the pavement not entirely sure what just happened.

The bus is not gone permanently. I know this. But knowing something and actually feeling it are two completely different things. Most days lately I am working harder on the feeling side than the knowing side.

The friends who got placed are careful around me now. They change the subject when placement comes up. I appreciate the intention. It also makes things slightly strange — like there is a topic-shaped gap in every conversation that everyone is quietly working around.

What Nobody Actually Sees When They Say "Still Not Placed"

When someone says those three words they are seeing a blank space. A result that has not arrived. A column that should have something in it and does not.

They are not seeing what lives inside that blank space.

They do not see the interviews I prepared for, traveled to, sat through nervous — and never heard back from. They do not see the rejection emails that arrived quietly at night when the house was already still. They do not see the hours spent rewriting a resume that nobody asked to see a second time, or the self-doubt that showed up every morning before I had done anything to deserve it, or the days I kept going not because I felt confident but because stopping felt worse than continuing.

None of that is visible. What is visible is only the outcome. The outcome has not arrived yet. But the effort has been real and it has been mine and I am not letting a casual question erase it.

My Mistakes — Said Plainly, Without Excuses

I could write entirely about external factors. The job market in 2026. The competition. The number of candidates per opening. All of that is real.

But there are also things I did — or did not do — that made this harder for myself. Saying them plainly is more useful than protecting myself from them.

The first one is that I had no clear direction for too long. I kept switching between Python one week, web development the next, data structures for a bit, random tutorials in between. Always learning something but never going deep enough into anything for it to actually matter when someone tests you on it. Moving between things felt like progress. It was not really progress.

The second is that I watched tutorials instead of building things. I finished a lot of courses. I genuinely felt like I was improving. Then I sat in front of a blank editor with no instructions and realized I could not build something from scratch without looking everything up. Following along with someone else's code is not the same as knowing the code. I confused those two things for much longer than I should have.

The third one is probably the most expensive. I kept waiting until I felt ready to apply. I told myself I would send applications after one more course, after one more project, after I felt more prepared. That feeling of readiness never fully came. Meanwhile time did not wait for me.

What I Am Doing Now

I stopped trying to feel ready before acting. I started doing things while not feeling ready. That is the whole shift. It sounds simple. It was not simple at all.

I am building real projects now — not tutorial projects. Things I built because I needed to understand something. Things I can explain in an interview without reading from memory.

I am going back to the basics. Data structures, algorithms, core Python — not to finish another course but to understand them well enough to actually use under pressure.

I am applying without waiting to feel prepared. Sending applications even on the days I feel underprepared. Every interview is information, not just a chance at an offer.

And I am writing here, publicly. It holds me to what I say I will do. It also means that someone looking for me can find me doing something rather than just being a name on a portal.

Getting placed is not about knowing everything. It is about showing what you know — clearly, with real evidence behind it. I did not fully understand that until recently. Now I do.

On the hard days I remind myself of a few things. This is a phase — it has a duration even if I do not know it yet. A rejection is data about a process, not a verdict on who I am. The people who got placed are not more capable; they are further along in a process I am still in. And writing this honestly is not weakness — it is exactly the kind of thing that makes someone else feel less alone at 2 AM.

Some Questions People Ask

Stop waiting to feel ready and start applying now. Build real projects and put them on GitHub. Write about what you are learning publicly. One visible consistent effort creates more opportunities than ten private ones. The applying is part of the preparing — not something that comes after it.

Yes. Many IT companies hire based on demonstrated skills, not degree background. Students from Biomedical, Mechanical, Civil and other non-CS branches have gotten placed in IT by learning Python, SQL and web development and showing real work on GitHub rather than depending only on their degree.

You cannot control what people ask. You can only control what you do after going home. Build something every day. Apply every day. Internal progress — even when invisible to everyone around you — builds up slowly and starts becoming louder than the external noise over time.

For Anyone Reading This at Night Who Is in the Same Place

If you found this because you searched for something about not being placed, or placement pressure, or just not knowing what to do next — you found it because someone else felt the same thing and decided to write it down instead of keeping it quiet.

You are not alone in this.

The people who are quiet about it are not doing better. They are just quieter. Those are two completely different things.

I do not have a success story to give you. I have not written that one yet. What I have is this — an honest account of being in the middle of something difficult, still going, still trying, not pretending everything is fine when it clearly is not.

I am still not placed. But I am not who I was three months ago either. And on most days, that is enough reason to keep going.

One day I will come back here and write an update. It will say something different. I do not know when. But I will — because I am not stopping.

I hope you do not stop either.

This is not a finished story. Neither am I.
